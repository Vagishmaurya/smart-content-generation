Understanding Democracy

What is democracy? The word 'democracy' comes from two Greek words: 'demos' meaning people, and 'kratia' meaning rule. So democracy literally means rule by the people. But what does this actually mean in practice? Let's explore the definition and history of democracy.

Democracy comes from the Greek word 'Demokratia' - 'demos' (people) and 'kratia' (rule). But what exactly does "rule by the people" mean in practice?

"Democracy is a form of government in which the rulers are elected by the people."

This simple definition helps us distinguish democracy from other forms of government where rulers are not chosen by the people:

Democracy

Government of the people, by the people, for the people

  • Rulers elected by people
  • Free and fair elections
  • One person, one vote
  • Rule of law

Monarchy

Rule by a king or queen, usually hereditary

  • Power inherited
  • No elections
  • Subjects, not citizens
  • Royal decrees

Dictatorship

Rule by one person with absolute power

  • No free elections
  • Suppression of opposition
  • Limited rights
  • Military control

But this simple definition is not enough. Many governments that hold elections are not truly democratic. We need to understand the key features that make a genuine democracy.

Evolution of Democracy

508 BCE
Athens introduces democracy - "rule by the people"

Athenian democracy was a direct democracy where male citizens gathered in the assembly to debate and vote on issues. Though limited to free men, it was revolutionary for its time.

1215
Magna Carta limits king's power in England

Though not democratic, this document established that the king was subject to the law, limiting absolute power and setting a foundation for future democratic development.

1789
French Revolution - "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity"

The revolution overthrew the monarchy and established principles of citizenship and equal rights, though full democracy was not immediately established.

1950
India becomes world's largest democracy

After gaining independence from British rule, India adopted a democratic constitution granting universal adult franchise from the very beginning - a remarkable achievement.

Essential Features of Democracy

Democracy isn't just about holding elections. A true democracy has several essential features that distinguish it from other forms of government. Let's explore these key features that make a system truly democratic.

Our simple definition of democracy as "rulers elected by the people" raises several questions:

  • Who are the rulers in this definition?
  • What kind of elections constitute democratic elections?
  • Who should be considered "the people" who can vote?
  • What limits should be placed on elected rulers?

These questions help us understand the key features of a genuine democracy:

Major Decisions by Elected Leaders

In a democracy, final decision-making power must rest with those elected by the people.

Example: In Pakistan under General Musharraf, people elected representatives but real power remained with unelected military officials. This was not a true democracy.

Free and Fair Electoral Competition

Elections must offer real choice between political alternatives and a fair chance for the ruling party to lose.

Example: In China, candidates need approval from the Communist Party. In Mexico until 2000, the PRI party used unfair practices to win every election.

One Person, One Vote, One Value

Each adult citizen must have one vote and each vote must have equal value regardless of their background.

Example: Until 2015, women in Saudi Arabia couldn't vote. In Fiji, indigenous Fijian votes had more value than Indian-Fijian votes.

Rule of Law and Respect for Rights

Democratic governments must operate within constitutional limits and respect citizens' rights.

Example: In Zimbabwe under Mugabe, elections were held but opposition was harassed, protests were banned, and court judgments were ignored.

To summarize, democracy is a form of government in which:

  1. Rulers elected by the people take all the major decisions;
  2. Elections offer a choice and fair opportunity to change the current rulers;
  3. This choice and opportunity is available to all people on an equal basis; and
  4. The exercise of this choice leads to a government limited by basic rules of the constitution and citizens' rights.

The Great Democracy Debate

Is democracy really the best form of government? In this section, we'll explore arguments both for and against democracy, considering its strengths and weaknesses from different perspectives.

Is democracy the best form of government? Explore arguments from both sides:

Arguments FOR Democracy

Accountability

Leaders must answer to people and can be changed peacefully

Equality

Every citizen has equal say regardless of status

Better Decisions

Multiple viewpoints lead to more balanced policies

Human Dignity

Respects citizens as equals, not subjects

Arguments AGAINST Democracy

Slow Decision Making

Consultation and debate can delay urgent decisions

Not everyone understands complex issues they vote on

Political Competition

Focus on winning elections rather than good governance

Instability

Frequent changes in leadership and policies

Why Choose Democracy?

Despite its flaws, democracy is widely regarded as the best form of government. Let's explore the key reasons why democracy is considered superior to other systems, even with its imperfections.

Democracy is not perfect. It can be slow, messy, and sometimes appears inefficient. So why do we still consider it the best form of government?

Accountable Government

Democracy is better at responding to people's needs. Non-democratic governments may respond to people's needs, but only if rulers want to. Democratic governments must respond or risk being voted out.

Better Decision Making

Democracy involves consultation and discussion among many people, reducing the chances of rash or irresponsible decisions. While this takes time, the quality of decisions improves.

Managing Differences

Democracy provides peaceful ways to handle conflicts in diverse societies. Different groups can live together because democracy ensures no one is a permanent winner or loser.

Human Dignity

Democracy enhances citizens' dignity by treating everyone as equals. Even the poorest and least educated have the same status as the rich and educated.

Self-Correction

Democracy allows for the correction of its own mistakes. Mistakes can't be hidden for long, and there's space for public discussion and correction.

Think About It

"Democracy is the worst form of government, except for all the others that have been tried."
- Winston Churchill

Democracy cannot get us everything and is not the solution to all problems. But it is clearly better than any other alternative that we know. It offers better chances of a good decision, it is likely to respect people's own wishes and allows different kinds of people to live together. Even when it fails to do these things, it allows a way of correcting its mistakes and offers more dignity to all citizens.
